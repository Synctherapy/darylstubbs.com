#### Light Output and the Science Behind It

The Mini 2.0 utilizes the exact same 660nm (red) and 850nm (near-infrared) wavelengths as Kala’s massive clinical panels. These specific wavelengths sit perfectly within the optical window for photobiomodulation (PBM). This means the light penetrates human tissue efficiently without getting blocked by water or blood.

Inside your cells, mitochondria absorb these photons through an enzyme called cytochrome c oxidase. This interaction triggers a massive spike in ATP (adenosine triphosphate) production. More ATP means your cells have the raw energy required to repair damaged tissue and clear out metabolic waste.

This process also stimulates the release of nitric oxide. Nitric oxide acts as a powerful vasodilator, expanding blood vessels to flood the injured area with oxygen and nutrients. I see the clinical results of this enhanced circulation every day in the form of reduced localized inflammation.

The 660nm red light targets superficial tissues. I recommend it for skin health, surface-level inflammation, and accelerating wound closure. The 850nm near-infrared light penetrates much deeper. It reaches dense muscle bellies, thick joint capsules, and bone tissue.

Clinical literature backs this up. A 2016 review by Ferraresi, Huang, and Hamblin (*J Biophotonics*, PMCID: PMC5167494) demonstrated that pre-exercise PBM significantly reduces muscle damage markers. While the Mini 2.0 won't cover your whole body, applying it to a specific strained muscle yields robust, science-backed recovery benefits.

Patients often tell me how effective the Mini is for stubborn, localized issues. From nagging patellar tendonitis to chronic wrist pain, the ability to precisely target the inflamed tissue makes this device incredibly valuable.
